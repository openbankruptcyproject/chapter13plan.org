The Chapter 13 Repayment Plan

A plain-language guide to how Chapter 13 bankruptcy plans work -- what goes in them, how payments are calculated, what the court requires for confirmation, and what happens when things go wrong.

What Is a Chapter 13 Plan?

A Chapter 13 plan is a court-supervised repayment proposal. The debtor proposes a plan to repay some or all debts over a period of three to five years. Creditors can object, the Chapter 13 trustee reviews the plan for compliance, and ultimately the bankruptcy judge decides whether to confirm it.

Unlike Chapter 7 -- which liquidates non-exempt assets and discharges most debts in a few months -- Chapter 13 lets you keep your property while catching up on missed payments over time. You keep your house, your car, and your paycheck. In exchange, you commit to a structured repayment plan funded by your disposable income.

Chapter 13 is often the only option for debtors who earn too much to qualify for Chapter 7 under the means test, or who need to cure a mortgage arrearage to save their home from foreclosure. It is also the only chapter that allows you to strip off wholly unsecured junior liens on your primary residence.

What Goes in the Plan?

A Chapter 13 plan must address every category of debt you owe. The plan divides claims into three tiers, each with different treatment:

  • Priority debts -- these must be paid in full. Priority debts include recent income taxes (generally the last three years), domestic support obligations (child support and alimony), and certain other claims given priority by the Bankruptcy Code. There is no negotiation here -- 100% payment is required for confirmation.
  • Secured debts -- these are debts backed by collateral. The plan addresses your mortgage (including curing any arrearage over the life of the plan), car loans, and other secured claims. For vehicles purchased more than 910 days before filing, you can "cram down" the loan to the vehicle's current value -- paying only what the car is worth, not what you owe.
  • Unsecured debts -- credit cards, medical bills, personal loans, and deficiency balances. These creditors receive whatever is left after priority and secured claims are paid. In many cases, unsecured creditors receive only pennies on the dollar -- sometimes nothing at all.

The plan must also account for the trustee's commission (typically 7-10% of payments) and any attorney fees not paid upfront. Most districts use a model plan form that standardizes the format, but the substance varies case by case.

How Payments Are Calculated

Your monthly plan payment is driven by the disposable income test under 11 U.S.C. Section 1325(b). If any unsecured creditor objects to the plan, you must commit all of your "projected disposable income" to the plan for the applicable commitment period.

Below-median debtors: If your household income is below the state median for your family size, your commitment period is three years (36 months). Disposable income is calculated using your actual monthly income minus actual monthly expenses -- Schedule I minus Schedule J on the bankruptcy forms.

Above-median debtors: If your income exceeds the state median, the commitment period extends to five years (60 months). Your disposable income is calculated using the means test (Form 122C-2), which applies IRS-standardized expense allowances rather than your actual spending. This can result in a higher calculated payment than what you actually spend.

The plan payment must also satisfy the "best interest of creditors" test -- unsecured creditors must receive at least as much as they would have received in a Chapter 7 liquidation. If you have significant non-exempt assets, this floor can push your payment higher than the disposable income calculation alone.

Plan Confirmation

Before your plan takes effect, the bankruptcy court must confirm it. Section 1325(a) sets out the requirements. The main ones are:

  • Good faith -- the plan must be proposed in good faith and not by any means forbidden by law. Courts look at the totality of circumstances, including whether the debtor is making a genuine effort to repay.
  • Best interest of creditors -- unsecured creditors must receive at least as much as they would get in a Chapter 7 liquidation. This is the "liquidation test."
  • Feasibility -- the debtor must be able to actually make the proposed payments. The court will not confirm a plan that sets payments the debtor cannot sustain. If your budget is too tight, the plan fails this test.
  • Full payment of priority claims -- all priority debts must be paid in full through the plan unless the holder agrees otherwise.

The Chapter 13 trustee reviews every plan before the confirmation hearing. The trustee checks the math, verifies income and expense figures against tax returns and pay stubs, and files an objection if anything does not add up. Creditors can also object -- secured creditors often challenge the valuation of their collateral, and unsecured creditors may argue the debtor is not committing enough income.

Confirmation hearings are typically scheduled 30-45 days after the 341 meeting of creditors. If the plan is not confirmable, the court may give the debtor time to file a modified plan rather than dismissing the case outright.

Modifying Your Plan

Life does not stop during a three-to-five-year repayment plan. Section 1329 allows the debtor, the trustee, or an unsecured creditor to request a plan modification after confirmation. Common reasons include:

  • Job loss or income reduction -- if you lose your job or take a pay cut, you can ask the court to lower your monthly payment.
  • Medical emergency -- unexpected medical expenses can be grounds for modifying expenses or extending the plan.
  • Change in household size -- a divorce, a new dependent, or a spouse losing income can all affect disposable income.
  • New debts -- post-petition debts (like a necessary car repair) may need to be addressed through modification.

Plan modifications must still satisfy the confirmation requirements of Section 1325(a). The modified plan cannot extend beyond five years from the date of the first payment under the original plan. Modifications are not guaranteed -- the court can deny them if the modified plan does not meet the statutory requirements.

What Happens If You Fall Behind?

Missing plan payments is the single most common reason Chapter 13 cases fail. When a debtor falls behind, the Chapter 13 trustee will typically file a motion to dismiss the case. Once dismissed, the automatic stay lifts and creditors can resume collection -- foreclosure, repossession, lawsuits, wage garnishment.

Before the case is dismissed, you have options:

  • Cure the arrearage -- catch up on missed payments before the hearing on the trustee's motion.
  • Modify the plan -- file a modified plan under Section 1329 that accounts for the changed circumstances.
  • Convert to Chapter 7 -- if the plan is no longer feasible, converting to Chapter 7 may allow you to discharge unsecured debts through liquidation instead.
  • Hardship discharge -- in rare cases, if you have already paid unsecured creditors at least as much as they would have received in a Chapter 7, and the failure to complete the plan is due to circumstances beyond your control (serious illness, disability), the court may grant a hardship discharge under Section 1328(b).

The trustee's motion to dismiss is not automatic -- it requires a court hearing, and the debtor has the right to respond. But courts move quickly on these motions, and debtors who do not respond risk losing the case by default.

Chapter 13 Completion Rates

Nationally, only about 33% of Chapter 13 cases result in a discharge. The other two-thirds are dismissed before the debtor finishes the plan -- meaning the debtor goes through months or years of payments and still does not get the fresh start bankruptcy promises.

Completion rates vary dramatically by attorney. Some attorneys consistently guide 50-60% of their clients to discharge. Others have discharge rates below 10%. The difference often comes down to case selection, plan feasibility, and ongoing client communication.
